X-ray Reflection Modeling with Fenrir

My most recent work has been focused on trying to better interpret the X-ray reflection spectra of supermassive black holes in active galactic nuclei (AGN), which is produced when the inner-most regions of the accretion disk are irradiated by a compact corona most commonly interpreted as the base of a black hole jet. These X-rays carry with it information about the central object, but unfortunately due to astrophyical uncertainties and the complexity of the physics, it is common to make simplifying assumptions in our models. This results in inaccuraties in the answers we infer from our data. For example, it is commonly assumed that the disk has negligible geometric thickness, an assumption that, while helpful in simplifying calculations, seems inconsistent with modern accretion theory.

In order to incorporate more accurate physics, I have created a new reflection model from scratch using the C++ and Python programming languages. Named Fenrir , this model traces the paths of photons through spacetime to predict the shape of the X-ray spectral features and allows for the use of any axisymmetric analytic disk model. Early results from this project have been presented in Taylor & Reynolds (2018a,b), which used a disk profile consistent with Shakura & Sunyaev (1973), suggest that disk geometry is indeed a major source of systematic error that had previously been largely ignored in the literature. For example, the inner-most accretion disk can act to shield the outer disk from the central corona, and thus would dominate the observed flux (see figure).

Fenrir will be released publicly as a pre-computed XSPEC grid model suite very soon (Taylor, Reynolds, & Reynolds in prep) and the corresponding code will be released shortly thereafter on my Github account . Afterward, I will use this new model to study a number of archetypical AGN, as well as perform a parameter space exploration to better quantify the systematic error inherent in using past models.
